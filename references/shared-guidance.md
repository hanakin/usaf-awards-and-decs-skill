# Shared Statement Mechanics

Use this reference for shared behavior that applies across the performance-brief lane and `AF Form 1206` unless a lane-specific file overrides it. In this skill, `EPB` and `OPB` are treated as interchangeable terms unless the user later requires a distinction.

## Glossary

- **Action Verb**: A verb that describes what the individual did. Use strong, specific verbs that draw attention without overstating the fact pattern.
- **Impact Verb**: A verb that describes the result or effect of the action. Use it to show consequence, not just activity.
- **ALQ**: The five Airmen Leadership Qualities used to route and evaluate statement content.
- **EPB / OPB**: Interchangeable performance-report terms in this skill's current operating model.
- **Statement**: A standalone sentence or two describing an accomplishment through action plus impact, result, or outcome.
- **Metrics**: Quantitative information such as counts, percentages, time spans, rates, workloads, or scale indicators. Use exact values, not approximations.
- **Quality Ranking**: The ordered ALQ suffix used by some formats to show which qualities the statement demonstrates most strongly.
- **Character Count**: The total number of characters in the statement string, including spaces, punctuation, symbols, and any required prefix.

## ALQ routing

| Abbreviation | Quality | Sub Quality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| E | Executing The Mission | Job Proficiency or Knowledge, Initiative, Adaptability |
| L | Leading People | Inclusion and Teamwork, Emotional Intelligence, Communication |
| M | Managing Resources | Stewardship, Accountability |
| I | Improving The Unit | Critical Thinking or Decision Making, Innovation |
| H | Higher Level Review | Most impactful accolade or accomplishment |

## Core statement rules

- Build each statement around one action and one impact, result, or outcome
- Keep the statement factual, supportable, and plain language
- Keep the member name near the front
- Use exact metrics whenever available
- Do not estimate missing numbers
- Use strong action and impact verbs without inflating the claim
- Avoid repeating the same words, phrasing, and verb choices across options

## Default placeholder identity

- Use `SSgt Peter Snuffy` as the default generic identity
- Do not ask the user for real personal details unless they explicitly want them included

## Approved name formats

- `First, Last`
- `First Last`
- `Rank Last`
- `Rank First Last`

When no real name is required, default to `SSgt Peter Snuffy`.

## Useful opening patterns

- Standard: `SrA Smith did something...`
- Role-based: `As the RFI manager, SrA Smith...`
- Problem-based: `Recognizing a gap in information sharing, SrA Smith...`

Use the role-based form only when the source material gives a real title or role.

## Metrics discipline

- Ask once for missing metrics when they materially affect impact
- If the user does not have more numbers, use the strongest supported facts available
- Prefer exact counts, time spans, percentages, and scale indicators
- Do simple math when the source facts support a defensible derived metric

## Variation guidance

- Produce materially different options, not verb swaps
- Vary structure, information order, and phrasing
- Use a thesaurus carefully for stronger or shorter alternatives
- Do not use a thesaurus for names, proper nouns, or approved acronyms

## Shortening tactics

When a statement is too long:

- Replace long approved phrases with approved acronyms
- Remove redundant setup language
- Use tighter verbs
- Compress number formatting
- Keep the most important metric and mission effect first

## Common failure points

- weak verbs
- repeated words
- vague metrics
- unsupported claims
- poor name format
- clarity sacrificed for compression

## Common pitfalls to avoid

- Do not overuse weak verbs like `improved`, `increased`, `enhanced`, or `supported`
- Do not repeat the same words inside one statement when a stronger synonym or restructure would fix it
- Do not settle for vague scale when exact numbers exist
- Do not forget required prefixes or required name placement
- Do not use unapproved acronyms
- Do not let length pressure make the sentence confusing
- Do not write generic template language that could fit anyone
- Do not describe activity without showing why it mattered
- Do not ignore grammar and flow during compression
- Do not use a thesaurus blindly and drift away from the underlying fact pattern

## Authority boundaries

- Use `Acronyms.md` for approved acronyms and shorthand
- Use `AFH 33-337` for grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and numbers
- Use lane-specific guidance for hard length rules and output structure
